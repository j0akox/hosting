Santos chilenos

Varied are the demonstrations of popular fervor recorded throughout history for these. To this it is necessary to add that the only ones to be recognized as saints by the Catholic Church are the Discalced Carmelite Teresa de
Los Andes and the Jesuit Alberto Hurtado.

Santa Teresa de Los Andes

Juana Enriqueta Josefina de los Sagrados Corazones Fernández Solar, venerated by the Catholic Church as Saint Teresa of Los Andes (Santiago, July 13, 1900-Los Andes, Valparaíso, April 12, 1920), was a Chilean Catholic nun. ,
belonging to the Discalced Carmelites.

She was beatified by John Paul II on April 3, 1987, during her only visit to Chile, and later canonized by the same pontiff in Saint Peter's Basilica (Vatican City) on March 21, 1993, when she was she became the first person born
in Chile, the first American Discalced Carmelite and the fourth Teresa of Carmel —after the Saints of Ávila, Florencia and
Lisieux— to be elevated to the altars. His feast is celebrated on July 13.

She was born into a wealthy family, the daughter of Miguel Fernández Jaraquemada and Lucía Solar Armstrong.1 She was baptized in
the Santa Ana parish in the city of Santiago in 1900, with the names of Juana Enriqueta Josefina de los Sagrados Corazones Fernández Solar,
but everyone in her family —formed by her parents, her three brothers, Miguel, Luis and Ignacio, and two
sisters, Lucía and Rebeca (later also a Discalced Carmelite), her maternal grandfather, Eulogio Solar; uncles, aunts, and cousins—called her Juanita.

He studied at the Colegio del Sagrado Corazón in Santiago. Between his studies, family life and his apostolate of
charity with the poorest, his intense love for Jesus Christ developed. At the age of fourteen, she decided to consecrate herself to God as a
Discalced Carmelite nun. She entered the monastery of the Holy Spirit of the Discalced Carmelites of Los Andes, in the diocese
of San Felipe de Aconcagua (Valparaíso Region) on May 7, 1919. On October 14, she took the habit and received the
name of Teresa de Jesús, thus beginning her novitiate year, at the end of which she would make her first temporary religious profession
which, given the circumstances, she was allowed to make before she died.

She had been in the convent for eleven months when she died of typhus and diphtheria at 7:15 p.m. on April 12, 1920, at the age of nineteen.2 Before she died, she professed as a Carmelite nun in articulo mortis.
It was initially buried in the convent cemetery and in 1940 it was transferred to the lower choir, next to the new large chapel.

San Alberto Hurtado

Luis Alberto Miguel Hurtado Cruchaga, revered by the Catholic Church as Saint Alberto Hurtado (Viña del Mar, January 22, 1901-Santiago, August 18, 1952), was a Chilean lawyer, trade unionist and Jesuit priest. He was popularly known for being the founder

of Hogar de Cristo. He is considered the patron of workers in Chile, co-patron (along with San Alfonso María de Ligorio) of the Faculty of Law and patron of the School of Social Work of his house of studies (Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile), of the
Federation of Chemical Engineering Students of the UCA and the University Ministry of Mendoza in Argentina.

He was beatified by John Paul II on October 16, 1994 and later canonized by Benedict XVI in Saint Peter's Square (Vatican City) on October 23, 2005, when he became the second person born in Chile — after Santa Teresa de Los Andes—in being elevated to the altars. His feast is celebrated on August 18.
